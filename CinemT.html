CinemAll App

Role UX Designer
Designer Team Ruturaj, Christa, Danielle
Type School Group Project

This project aims at improving the in-theater movie experience for visually impaired moviegoers who currently need theater-provided descriptive video devices to enjoy a movie. I worked as a UX Designer for 3 months, mainly responsible for interviewing cinema staff, concept drawing for the physical kiosk idea, storyboard drawing to visualize touchpoints between three initial concepts and the defined users, qualitative and quantitative data analysis for the Round 2 usability testing and high fidelity prototype iterations.

Problem Statement

In-theater Movie Watching Is Not Universal

Theaters and movies are no longer new tech that makes people crazy. According to Statistics, 13 percent of Americans go to the movies about once a month. However, we discovered a group of neglected customers whose in-theater experience is far from perfect -- the visually impaired moviegoers. This project aims at improving the in-theater movie experience for the visually impaired moviegoers who currently need theater-provided descriptive video devices to enjoy a movie.

Our hypothesis is that by improving the visually impaired moviegoers' movie experience, theaters can increase ticket sales and establish stronger brand loyalty with a wider customer base.

An Immersive Experience Aided by DVS

Through literature review, we discovered that despite of their impaired vision, the immersive in-theater experience, created by actors, story, sounds and the music, are the main reasons drive them to the theater.

Required by The Twenty-First Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act of 2010, theaters are providing an accessible service to the visually impaired moviegoers, the Descriptive Video Service (DVS), a narrative audio track that descibes the movies' key visual elements and fits in natural pauses between lines of diaglogs in the film..

Key Insights

Against their wish of being independent, the renting process requires the visually impaired rely on theater associates inevitably.

Before the movie, the visually impaired moviegoers have to arrive earlier than others with normal vision, line in front of service desk, request the DVS headsets from certain associates, and then leave their personal information as an exchange.

Complaint by the visually impaired, this renting process leaves an impression to them that they need a special care and are being suspected. Not to mention the extra time and effort the visually impaired spend to find the right associates, most of which usually struggle to set the right DVS streaming channel.

Many service provider like Apple strives to promote independence for the visually impaired through features like Voice Over (in the left). Acquiring headsets from theater associates still remain as an inevitable step when the visually impaired need DVS service.

Limited by hardware technology, the malfunction of the DVS headsets often disrupt the watching experience, which is supposed to be immersive.

Unstable streaming signal and malfunction of the DVS headsets usually happen when users are enjoying their movie. The theater lack a way to prevent that and have to require the visually impaired to go back to the service desk and change to another one. When the users come back, they often miss a big portion of the movie, which is claimed as frustrating.

Secret Cinema is pioneering in creating immersive real scene experience for moviegoers, inviting them to dressup and interact with actors as the plots develop(in the left). However, the visually impaired moviegoers have to leave their seat when the DVS headsets are not working in the mid of a traditional movie (in the right).

It happens a lot... in the mid of the movie, I have to squeeze out of the seating area and let the associate fix headset problems. It takes time to fix it and when I come back, there are always some scenes missing...

— One Participant

Potential Design Directions

One Kiosk, two Apps

Our group went through the first round brainstorming and filtered out three TOP ideas through Feasible vs Impact Chart, two of my ideas were included, the smart kiosk idea and request DVS app idea. After the first wireframe session, I then quickly generated three storyboards to visualize users' new movie watching journeys for these concepts. This methods helps the team to build empathy and critique towards new concepts.

Through the journey we discovered that Concpet 2 can not only promote indepence by reducing associate-customer interaction to the minimum, but also less likely to fail, leverage on users' own maintainance.

Learn From Competitors

New Released Movie Access

While streaming DV app seems a fairly viable solution, we still conducted a competitor analysis to Actiview, the only app on market for DVS launched in 2017, March (four months before our project). I analyzed through the app reviews and discovered that many customers mentioned they expected more content while achieving a 4.8 out of 5 rating.

Through group discussion, we believe that streaming the audio track has the benefits to provide new-released movie DVS, preventing the content leakage issue brought by downloading, thus not violating theaters' interest. Also, new released movie is a key element that drives customers to the theather. Meeting the demands of both theaters and customers, we believe that streaming feature is a key function to promote theather ticket sales and establish brand loyalty.

Design Iterations

Main Flow

We assume that the target users have been familiar with the process to rent descriptive audio and used the physical process as a reference to segment our task flow. To reduce users need to choose both theather and cinema, we assume that Ticket QR code scan is a suggested to reduce user operations.

Expert Evaluation

Cognitive Walkthrough

We invited the experts in the Visually Impaired Center to do a evaluation on the app. Reported by them, it can be very promising in this field. But one thing they mentioned is that unlinke what we assumed that all the visually impaired would use the Voice Over function, for slightly visually impaired, they tend to use magnifiers. This arises new questions like how can we design the app under magnified view.

Reflections

What I learned from the Projects

Let users talk. At the very begining, we were trapped with our own experience and had a hard time identifying a valueable key points. However, when we went out and talked with the real users, their stories inspired us a lot. I really appreciate that when we were doing the pilot interview, Christa asked the participants, "Besides eating, is there other scenarios that you were experiencing?" And when the visually impaired participants tell her movie stories, we were so excited and inspired. Talk with users allow us to break the border of designers' own life and gain empathy towards our users.

Considering the context when designing tasks. This App serves for a whole process from purchasing movie tickets to walk out the theater. Different contexts have high influence on how each stakeholder behavea. For example, the manager at the Regal Cinema told us, they wouldn't provide service prior the arrival of the users. However, the visually impaired users would love to be prepared before their arrival. Considering different context limitations throughout the process is such a great lesson for me.
