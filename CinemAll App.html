CinemAll App

The Challenge

To enjoy a movie, the visually impaired moviegoers currently need to rent theater-provided descriptive video devices , which often have technical problems that disrupt an immersive movie watching experience.

Movie watching experience for the visually impaired is less ideal.
Our Hypothesis

We hypothesis that this mobile app will help to improve the theater ticket sales by encouraging more visually impaired moviegoers to the theaters, and thus build a stronger brand loyalty for theater companies like Regal, Landmark and so on.

Our Solution

Rather than utilizing the in-theater DVS headsets, by leveraging on users’ own mobile phone, we should be able to improve the immersive movie watching experience in reducing customer-staff interaction and the possibility of device malfunction.

Key Features

Rather than utilizing the in-theater DVS headsets, by leveraging on users’ own mobile phone, we should be able to improve the immersive movie watching experience in reducing customer-staff interaction and the possibility of device malfunction.

One Tap To Access to DVS
Users are looking for the quickest way to access to their DVS. By colloborating with theater partners like Landmark, Regal, AMC and so on, we aim to obtain ticket purchase history to reduce users' input. On openning the app, the DVS that they require is right at hand.
Multiple Methods to Add New DVS
For users who purchase offline or didn't find the synced movie ticket due to potential error, several methods are provided to gurantee that users can request the DVS as soon as possible.
Theater Help for Emergency
The app also leveraging on theater help to solve all types of emergency, a conversation based option is able to reduce the panic in situations where users don't know how to fix an error.

1.3 Affinity Diagram

We conducted an interpretation of the previous user interviews. Then we used the Affinity Mapping to create a visual representation of the problem space and see if any patterns emerged. And the following problem patterns emerged: 1) Visually impaired moviegoers want to be prepared when they go to a movie theater. 2)Visually impaired moviegoers have difficulty acquiring appropriate DV devices in theater. 3) Visually impaired moviegoers struggle managing situations when their devices malfunction.

02. Design Direction Identification

In-theater Audio Streaming App, cutting out existing devices

This solution aims to cut down on the learning curve by using the device users already have with them, their smartphone. Users won’t need to lug around additional clunky devices. This solution will also cut out the need to interact with theater associates who often provide incorrectly configured devices for the users.

Comparing different prototype tools, including Axure, Framer, Invision and JustInMind, we discovered that JustInMind has the function to include audio files to the prototype.

Design Alternative 1 - Smart Kiosk
This solution will aim to cut out interaction with theater associates, who are often times undertrained and cause unnecessary frustrations for visually impaired moviegoers. Additionally, we envision the kiosks to be a universal solution and a one-stop shop for all moviegoers to purchase or pick-up tickets and retrieve assistive devices.

Design Alternative 2- In-theater Service App, leveraging existing devices
This solution aims to be low cost, as theaters won’t have to replace their devices. Here, instead we will pair users with devices before they arrive at the theater, therefore alleviating frustrations of interacting with under-trained staff.

2.4 Main Flow Design Iterations

Because our users are visually impaired, we did some preliminary testing to confirm that our colors and contrasts meet widely-accepted guidelines. We used the WebAIM web app to check our contrast ratios. We considered a 4.5:1 contrast ratio as passing based off of the w3 recommendations for accessibility[2]. Red was used because it has the longest wavelength, and provides the least stress on the eye when switching between it and dark contexts.

Additionally, we made sure to add patterned backgrounds to all clickable elements to double-encode the information so that users with poor color perception can easily distinguish different elements on the screens. Lastly, we made the text and buttons large to allow for user error when aiming, which might be more common among our users.

4. Design Iterations

Allocate the Task to Reduce User Input

Through user testing, we discovered that users prefer some quick setup method to match them with the right movie. Several iterations were made to better convey this idea on the home page, which is an important anchor to user expectation.

High Contrast Color that Convey Right Emotion

We made several color testing to find a color that convey a confirmation meaning as well as conveying the highest contrast.

Reduce Finger Motions

For visually impaired who utilized voice-over, we discovered that finger movement is much more frequent than ordinary people. The visually impaired has to use the finger to activate the voice-over function to hear the description of interface elements. In the final design, two finger movement patterns were introduced to reduce back and forth finger movement.

05 Reflection

Considering the context when designing tasks. This App serves for a whole process from purchasing movie tickets to walk out the theater. Different contexts have high influence on how each stakeholder behave. For example, the manager at the Regal Cinema told us, they wouldn't provide service prior the arrival of the users. However, the visually impaired users would love to be prepared before their arrival. Considering different context limitations throughout the process is such a great lesson for me.
